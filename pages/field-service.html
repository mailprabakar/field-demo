Field Service Management During COVID 19.

It is at the core of every utility provider to fulfill and exceed customer expectations in Field Services Management. However, the traditional system of Field service management fails to meet up with these expectations. This is largely due to its inefficiencies in processes and operations.

In this system, operations such as logging, monitoring, and control of numerous variables are performed by human labor and prone to much error. Also, tracking all unprecedented changes on-site demands a significant amount of time and precision.

Among other things, delays, miscommunications, incorrect and incomplete information are common drawbacks of the system. Notable changes began to occur in the wake of the increased adoption of mobile devices and connected systems. Many Field Service Management companies adopted mobile apps and similar FSM technologies or software that assisted them in providing better services to customers.

Unfortunately, a novel set of challenges arose with the global COVID-19 pandemic. Currently, FSM contractors are saddled with keeping technicians and customers safe as well as providing essential field services.

To combat these challenges, experts have suggested using certain guidelines, including efficient Field Service Management software, to reduce visits to the field.

In a poll conducted by TSIA on their State of Field Services webinar, 88% of the attendees admitted that Field Services Engineer deployments had been impacted

Some of the challenges of the Field Services Management industry amid the COVID-19 pandemic are as belows

  • Limited On-site service delivery: Before the outbreak of the pandemic, field technicians worked with a schedule and were assigned to specific on-site jobs. They were also expected to maintain assignments to meet customers’ expectations. However, with the imposition of the work from home mandates, travel bans, closure of offices, and other restrictions due to the pandemic, field engagements have drastically reduced. Contractors are concerned about the safety and health of employees and customers. As a result, on-site jobs of technicians are limited to critical cases only.
  • Emerging Technology: Utility providers who are reluctant to embrace new and emerging technology face several challenges, especially in this time of crisis. The world is evolving, and field service management services have to evolve to remain relevant and provide effective services to customers. Sadly, many utility providers find it hard to abandon antiqued software, materials, specifications, and procedures because of the time and money they invested. A typical example is the use of papers to log and document field information instead of easy to use, time-saving digital devices that will prevent loss of vital information, and properly organize workflow.
  • Safety and Liability: The safety of field workers is one of the most pressing concerns of field managers, particularly in this pandemic. This is because there are serious cases where dispatching technicians to the field is inevitable. In such situations, the health of these employees might be jeopardized. Also, employees are likely to quit their jobs if they feel their safety is not prioritized. Therefore, it has become important for FSx companies to invest in safety tools and techniques to protect employees.
  • Lack of communication with Field Technicians: It is imperative for supervisors to constantly track technicians while on the field to enable information flow. Among other things, communication between field workers and back-office is important for implementing necessary changes during operations. However, real-time communication is often hindered by several factors, including weak network signals, a prominent feature of remote areas.
